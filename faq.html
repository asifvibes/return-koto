The data & the verified badge

What does the verified badge mean, and how is it decided?

A fund is marked verified when its return is built from full, sourced data: its weekly NAV history plus every cash dividend, taken from the asset manager's official disclosures, with the fee, sponsor, trustee and custodian details read directly from its BSEC prospectus.

When part of that data is incomplete, the fund carries a flag instead, with the exact reason shown on its row. Partial means some dividend records are missing, so the total may be understated (IDLC Growth, IDLC Shariah and ICB Islami are examples). Estimated means a figure is modelled and labelled as such: MTB's undeclared middle-year dividends are set to its consistent rate of about 14%, and the five VIPB funds are anchored to VIPB's own officially published total returns, because VIPB does not disclose year-by-year dividends. Nothing is shown that we cannot trace to a source, and where a prospectus is not available the fund shows limited details rather than anything unverified.

How does ReturnKoto calculate what my money would have done?

For each fund we take its real NAV history as weekly snapshots and replay your investment. A lump sum buys units once at the starting NAV. A monthly SIP buys units every month at that month's NAV, and cash dividends are reinvested to buy more units. "Max" uses each fund's full available history, so the start date differs by fund. All figures use each fund's published NAV, which is already net of the fund's management fees.

Does the return include dividends?

Yes. Every figure is a total return that counts both the change in unit price and each fund's cash dividends, reinvested, sourced from the asset managers. The engine matches the asset managers' own published return figures (for example IDLC Income and Shanta) to within about one percent.

What is the annualized figure?

It is CAGR (compound annual growth rate) for a lump sum, and money-weighted IRR for a SIP, which accounts for the timing of each monthly deposit. Both express a multi-year result as a single yearly percentage. Weekly NAVs are updated automatically every Friday. Past performance does not guarantee future results.

Common questions

How are mutual fund returns calculated on ReturnKoto?

ReturnKoto replays your investment over each fund's real NAV history (weekly snapshots) and reinvests cash dividends, so every figure is a total return. Lump sum buys units once at the starting NAV; monthly SIP buys units each month. Annualized returns use CAGR for lump sum and money-weighted IRR for SIP.

What fees do Bangladeshi open-end mutual funds charge?

Most charge an annual management fee, often tiered from about 2.5% down to 1% of net asset value as the fund grows, while a few charge a flat rate near 0.7% to 1%. Funds also pay small regulated trustee, custodian and BSEC fees. ReturnKoto shows each fund's exact fee, read from its official BSEC prospectus.

What is an exit load, and which funds charge one?

An exit load is a small fee deducted if you redeem early. It varies by fund: some charge a percentage if you redeem within a set window (for example 1% within 30 days, or 2% within 60 days), some only charge if a monthly SIP is cancelled within the first one to three years, and some charge nothing at all. Each fund's exact exit load, with its rate and time window, is shown in its details and on its own page.

Which Bangladeshi mutual fund has the best return?

It depends on the time period and whether you invest monthly or in a lump sum. ReturnKoto ranks every fund by historical total return for the settings you choose and marks the top performer. Past performance does not guarantee future results.

What is the difference between SIP and lump-sum investing?

A lump sum invests all your money at once. A SIP, or Systematic Investment Plan, invests a fixed amount every month, which spreads your entry price over time. ReturnKoto compares both side by side on real fund history.

Are there Shariah-compliant mutual funds in Bangladesh?

Yes. Several open-end funds are Shariah-compliant, and ReturnKoto has a one-tap filter to show only those. There is also a guide to Shariah funds.

Can I redeem open-end mutual funds any time?

Open-end funds are redeemable at net asset value on a regular basis, often weekly. Some funds apply a small early-exit charge if you redeem within a set window or cancel a SIP within the first one to three years. Each fund's liquidity and exit load are shown in its details.

How do I invest in a mutual fund in Bangladesh?

You first open a BO (Beneficiary Owner) account, since fund units are held in dematerialised form, then open an account with the asset management company or its authorised selling agent and buy units as a one-time lump sum or a monthly SIP. See the step-by-step how to invest guide. ReturnKoto shows each fund's historical returns and verified details so you can compare first; it does not sell funds.

What is the minimum amount needed to invest?

Many asset managers let you start a monthly SIP from around ৳1,000, while lump-sum minimums vary by fund. Each fund's stated minimum is listed on its own page.

Do mutual funds in Bangladesh offer a tax rebate?

Yes, and the 2026-27 budget made them more tax-friendly. It removed the old ৳5 lakh ceiling that capped how much of a mutual fund investment could earn a rebate, so a much larger investment now qualifies, up to the overall yearly rebate limit (up to ৳75 lakh of eligible investment, rebated at 10%). Individual dividend income is now taxed at a flat 15%. Tax rules change each year, so confirm the current figures with the NBR.

Can Non-Resident Bangladeshis (NRBs) invest in mutual funds?

Yes. NRBs can invest in many Bangladeshi open-end mutual funds; check each fund's prospectus for the exact eligibility and process.

What is the difference between open-end and closed-end mutual funds?

Open-end funds are bought and redeemed at net asset value directly with the fund, on a regular basis. Closed-end funds have a fixed number of units that trade on the stock exchange, where the market price can sit above or below NAV. ReturnKoto covers open-end funds.

What is NAV (Net Asset Value)?

NAV is the per-unit value of a fund's assets after its fees. You buy and redeem open-end units at NAV, and a fund's total return is the change in NAV plus its cash dividends, reinvested.

How is a mutual fund different from a DPS, Sanchayapatra or fixed deposit?

A DPS, Sanchayapatra or fixed deposit pays a fixed, pre-set rate. A mutual fund is market-linked, so its return varies and can be higher or lower. ReturnKoto shows each fund next to Sanchayapatra, DPS, FDR, gold and inflation so you can weigh the trade-off; there is also a detailed comparison guide.

Sources & data

Prices & NAV history. Each fund's net asset value is calculated and published by its own asset manager under BSEC rules, often daily, and disclosed publicly. ReturnKoto uses the weekly NAV to keep the calculation simple and comparable across funds. The multi-year history behind the calculator is compiled from those public disclosures and the market-data sources below.

Fund details: fees, exit loads, trustee, custodian, liquidity. Read directly from each fund's official prospectus filed with the Bangladesh Securities and Exchange Commission (BSEC), and cross-checked against the asset manager's own published fund pages where available. Only the figures the source actually states are shown; anything undisclosed is left out rather than guessed.

Safe-option benchmark rates. Sanchayapatra, DPS, FDR, inflation and gold figures are period averages from public sources: the Bangladesh Bureau of Statistics (inflation), Bangladesh Bank and the World Bank (deposit and FDR rates), the Department of National Savings (Sanchayapatra), and gold market prices. They update with the look-back period and stay editable; DPS has no single official rate, so it is approximate.

Notes. Scope is open-end mutual funds only, the kind you buy and redeem directly at net asset value; exchange-listed closed-end funds are not included.
