How this works +

What's covered. Only open-end mutual funds, the kind you can buy and redeem any day at net asset value (NAV). Exchange-listed closed-end funds are a different product and are not included here.

How this works. For each fund we take its real NAV history as weekly snapshots and replay your investment. Lump sum buys units once at the starting NAV. Monthly SIP buys units every month at that month's NAV, and cash dividends are reinvested to buy more units. "Max" uses each fund's full available history, so the start date differs by fund. All figures use each fund's published NAV, which is already net of the fund's management fees, and the fund size shown is its latest assets under management.

Total return, including dividends. Figures count both the unit-price change and each fund's cash dividends, reinvested, sourced from the asset managers. A few funds carry a caveat, flagged on their own row: MTB is marked estimated because its undeclared middle-year dividends are set to its consistent ~14% rate; the five VIPB funds are estimated too, anchored to VIPB's own officially published total returns since VIPB does not disclose year-by-year dividends; and the partial funds (IDLC Growth, IDLC Shariah, ICB Islami) have incomplete dividend records, so their totals may be understated. The engine matches the asset managers' own published return figures (for example IDLC Income and Shanta) to within about one percent. Funds built from full, sourced data are marked verified; the few that carry a caveat show the specific reasoning right on their row, so you can always see how we arrived at it.

Fund details. Tap "Fund details" on any fund for its management fee, asset manager, sponsor, trustee, custodian, liquidity, any early-exit charge, face value and minimum investment, each read directly from that fund's official BSEC prospectus. Every fund also has its own page, opened from its name in the list or from the Funds directory, with its returns by period, a NAV growth chart and full details. Where a prospectus isn't available, that fund shows limited details rather than anything unverified.

Annualized figure is CAGR for lump sum and money-weighted IRR for SIP. Weekly NAVs as of 18 Jun 2026, updated automatically every Friday. Past performance does not guarantee future results.

Common questions +

How are mutual fund returns calculated on ReturnKoto?
ReturnKoto? replays your investment over each fund's real NAV history (weekly snapshots) and reinvests cash dividends, so every figure is a total return. Lump sum buys units once at the starting NAV; monthly SIP buys units each month. Annualized returns use CAGR for lump sum and money-weighted IRR for SIP.

What fees do Bangladeshi open-end mutual funds charge?
Most charge an annual management fee, often tiered from about 2.5% down to 1% of net asset value as the fund grows, while a few charge a flat rate near 0.7% to 1%. Funds also pay small regulated trustee, custodian and BSEC fees. ReturnKoto? shows each fund's exact fee, read from its official BSEC prospectus.

Which Bangladeshi mutual fund has the best return?
It depends on the time period and whether you invest monthly or in a lump sum. ReturnKoto? ranks every fund by historical total return for the settings you choose and marks the top performer. Past performance does not guarantee future results.

What is the difference between SIP and lump-sum investing?
A lump sum invests all your money at once. A SIP, or Systematic Investment Plan, invests a fixed amount every month, which spreads your entry price over time. ReturnKoto? compares both side by side on real fund history.

Are there Shariah-compliant mutual funds in Bangladesh?
Yes. Several open-end funds are Shariah-compliant, and ReturnKoto? has a one-tap filter to show only those.

Can I see everything about one fund?
Yes. Every fund has its own page with its full returns by period, a NAV growth chart and verified details (fee, trustee, custodian and more). Open it from the fund's name in the list, or browse them all in the Funds directory.

Can I redeem open-end mutual funds any time?
Open-end funds are redeemable at net asset value on a regular basis (often weekly). Some funds apply a small early-exit charge if a SIP is cancelled within the first one to three years. Each fund's liquidity and any exit fee are shown in its details.

How do I invest in a mutual fund in Bangladesh?
You first open a BO (Beneficiary Owner) account, since fund units are held in dematerialised form, then open an account with the asset management company or its authorised selling agent and buy units as a one-time lump sum or a monthly SIP. ReturnKoto? shows each fund's historical returns and verified details so you can compare first; it does not sell funds.

What is the minimum amount needed to invest?
Many asset managers let you start a monthly SIP from around ৳1,000, while lump-sum minimums vary by fund. Each fund's stated minimum is listed on its own page.

Do mutual funds in Bangladesh offer a tax rebate?
Yes, and the 2026-27 budget made them more tax-friendly. It removed the old ৳5 lakh ceiling that capped how much of a mutual fund investment could earn a rebate, so a much larger investment now qualifies, up to the overall yearly rebate limit (up to ৳75 lakh of eligible investment, rebated at 10%). Individual dividend income is now taxed at a flat 15%. Tax rules change each year, so confirm the current figures with the NBR.

Can Non-Resident Bangladeshis (NRBs) invest in mutual funds?
Yes. NRBs can invest in many Bangladeshi open-end mutual funds; check each fund's prospectus for the exact eligibility and process.

What is the difference between open-end and closed-end mutual funds?
Open-end funds are bought and redeemed at net asset value (NAV) directly with the fund, on a regular basis. Closed-end funds have a fixed number of units that trade on the stock exchange, where the market price can sit above or below NAV. ReturnKoto? covers open-end funds.

What is NAV (Net Asset Value)?
NAV is the per-unit value of a fund's assets after its fees. You buy and redeem open-end units at NAV, and a fund's total return is the change in NAV plus its cash dividends, reinvested.

How is a mutual fund different from a DPS, Sanchayapatra or fixed deposit?
A DPS, Sanchayapatra or fixed deposit pays a fixed, pre-set rate. A mutual fund is market-linked, so its return varies and can be higher or lower. ReturnKoto? shows each fund next to Sanchayapatra, DPS, FDR, gold and inflation so you can weigh the trade-off.

Sources & data +

Prices & NAV history

Each fund's net asset value is calculated and published by its own asset manager under BSEC rules, often daily, and disclosed publicly. ReturnKoto? uses the weekly NAV to keep the calculation simple and comparable across funds. The multi-year history behind the calculator is compiled from those public disclosures and the market-data sources listed below.

Fund details: fees, trustee, custodian, liquidity

Read directly from each fund's official prospectus filed with the Bangladesh Securities and Exchange Commission (BSEC). Only the figures the prospectus actually states are shown; anything it doesn't disclose is left out rather than guessed.

Safe-option benchmark rates

Sanchayapatra, DPS, FDR, inflation and gold figures are period averages from public sources: the Bangladesh Bureau of Statistics (inflation), Bangladesh Bank and the World Bank (deposit and FDR rates), the Department of National Savings (Sanchayapatra), and gold market prices. They update with the look-back period and stay editable; DPS has no single official rate, so it is approximate.
